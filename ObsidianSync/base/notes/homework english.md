**5. What kind of political system does the UK have?**

**6. What is parliamentary democracy? What are the main peculiarities of this political system under a constitutional monarchy?**
Based on the text, this system means that while there is a Monarch who acts as the head of state, they do not hold the power to govern. The peculiarity is that the Monarch works ceremonially, while the actual decisions and the running of the government are handled by the Prime Minister. Additionally, the text notes that this system operates without a codified written constitution.

**7. What are the three branches of power in the UK? Talk about each of them.**
*   **Legislative power:** The power to make new laws or remove old ones. This is held by Parliament (House of Lords and House of Commons), as well as the Scottish Parliament and Welsh/Northern Irish Assemblies.
*   **Executive power:** The power to implement and enforce laws. This is controlled by the British government (working on behalf of the monarch) and the devolved governments of Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.
*   **Judicial power:** The power to prosecute those who break the law. This branch is independent of the legislature and executive, with the highest court being the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom.

**8. What chambers does the British Parliament consist of? What functions do they perform?**
Parliament consists of two chambers (a bicameral legislature):
*   **The House of Commons:** This is the elected body. It is responsible for funding government departments by passing tax laws.
*   **The House of Lords:** This is a non-elected body. It provides a second opinion to the Commons. Its main roles are making laws, checking/challenging the work of the government, and investigating public policy.
*   **Shared Functions:** Both Houses make laws, hold the government to account, and debate issues.

**9. What is uncodified constitution? What arguments do the supporters of the uncodified constitution give in favour of it?**
An uncodified constitution means that a nation's rules have never been written down together in a single legal document, and some rules have not been written down at all. Supporters argue that trying to reduce a nation's entire legal, political, ethical, and social rules into a single list risks **over-simplification**.
